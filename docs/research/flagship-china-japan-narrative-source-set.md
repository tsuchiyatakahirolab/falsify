# Flagship China-to-Japan narrative source set

Research date: 2026-07-17

## Recommendation

Use the reciprocal official narratives surrounding Japan's FY2026 defense budget as the flagship demonstration.

On 26 December 2025, China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs described the record Japanese defense budget and counterstrike buildup as evidence of remilitarization and a possible revival of militarism. On the same date, Japan's defense minister described the budget as the minimum needed for defense and alleged that portrayals of Japan as uniquely pursuing military expansion were intentional information warfare. The shared factual core is unusually strong; the competing intent, constitutional, historical-analogy, and causal claims are not established by the budget figure alone.

This is a better demonstration than a simple true/false dispute because Falsify can:

- confirm a fact conceded by both sides;
- distinguish a large defense buildup from the stronger historical analogy of "resurrecting militarism";
- apply the same evidence rule to reciprocal allegations of deception or information warfare;
- test Japan's "exclusively defense-oriented" description against actual counterstrike capabilities and domestic debate;
- add regional context without treating China's own military spending as proof that every Japanese policy choice is justified;
- revise or qualify its initial finding when the user invokes **Challenge this finding**.

Do not label either statement "disinformation." The sources do not establish deceptive intent.

## Primary narrative input

### CN-1

- Publisher: Ministry of Foreign Affairs of the People's Republic of China
- Title: `Foreign Ministry Spokesperson Lin Jian's Regular Press Conference on December 26, 2025`
- Date: 2025-12-26 (page updated 19:30 local time)
- Language: English (official English-language MFA page)
- URL: https://www.fmprc.gov.cn/mfa_eng/xw/fyrbt/202512/t20251226_11788128.html
- Source role: Official Chinese position and flagship input, not independent evidence for the position's factual assertions.
- Recommended input boundary: the answer beginning with the FY2026 budget question and ending with the statement about defending the outcomes of the Second World War. Do not include the unrelated next question.

Short paraphrased claim layers:

1. **Quantitative factual core:** Japan's FY2026 draft defense budget exceeded JPY9 trillion and set a record.
2. **Trend claim:** Japan is rapidly increasing its military capabilities.
3. **Capability claim:** Japan has expanded collective-self-defense policy, is acquiring counterstrike capability, and is strengthening its southwestern/frontier islands.
4. **Policy-fit claim:** These changes exceed Japan's exclusively defense-oriented policy.
5. **Domestic/international-law claim:** The changes breach Japan's Constitution and the postwar international order.
6. **Intent attribution:** Japanese right-wing forces intend to remilitarize the country and revive militarism.
7. **Deception attribution:** The terms "self-defense" and "counterstrike" are being used to mislead public opinion.
8. **Historical analogy:** Current changes are presented as a possible return of pre-1945 Japanese militarism.
9. **Causal/normative conclusion:** The defense buildup is moving Japan away from peace and in a dangerous direction.
10. **Public-response claim:** Japanese residents, scholars, and protesters have challenged the spending increase and military expansion.

## Symmetric Japanese official sources

### JP-1

- Publisher: Japan Ministry of Defense
- Title: `Press Conference by Defense Minister Koizumi on Friday, December 26, 2025, at 11:22 AM`
- Date: 2025-12-26
- Language: Japanese original; an English page exists but is explicitly a provisional external translation and currently exposes only part of the transcript.
- Japanese URL: https://www.mod.go.jp/j/press/kisha/2025/1226a.html
- English landing URL: https://www.mod.go.jp/en/article/2025/12/556e54b51b4a005759746cb6e005af5704c9f6a9.html
- Source role: Official Japanese response and policy explanation, not independent proof of defensive intent or legal compatibility.

Short paraphrased counter-claims:

1. The base budget was a record JPY8.8 trillion and exceeded JPY9 trillion when specified SACO and U.S.-forces realignment costs were included.
2. The budget was described as the minimum necessary in Japan's most difficult postwar security environment.
3. The minister said Japan would retain the Three Non-Nuclear Principles, an exclusively defense-oriented posture, and its peaceful-state course.
4. The budget includes personnel and living-condition spending as well as unmanned coastal defense, stand-off missiles, southwest-region organization, and space capability.
5. The minister alleged that accounts portraying only Japan as expanding its military, while omitting neighboring military growth, were intentional information warfare.

Symmetry rule: claim 5 is an intent attribution and should not receive a lower evidentiary bar than CN-1's deception attribution. The transcript itself does not establish the state of mind of all publishers making critical claims.

### JP-2

- Publisher: Japan Ministry of Defense
- Title: `Key Points of the FY2026 Draft Defense Budget` (`令和８年度防衛予算案のポイント`)
- Date: 2025-12-26 (publication date shown in the Ministry budget index)
- Language: Japanese
- URL: https://www.mod.go.jp/j/budget/yosan_gaiyo/fy2026/point.pdf
- Source role: Primary budget summary.
- Provenance note: It confirms the first-ever JPY9 trillion figure on the stated inclusive accounting basis, continuous growth from approximately JPY6.8 trillion in FY2023, and priorities including stand-off missiles and unmanned systems. The accounting footnote matters: a user should not compare the inclusive JPY9 trillion figure with a differently scoped series without qualification.

### JP-3

- Publisher: Cabinet Secretariat, Government of Japan
- Title: `National Security Strategy of Japan`
- Date: 2022-12-16 (approved by the National Security Council and Cabinet)
- Language: English
- URL: https://www.cas.go.jp/jp/siryou/221216anzenhoshou/national_security_strategy_2022_pamphlet-e.pdf
- Source index: https://www.cas.go.jp/jp/siryou/221216anzenhoshou.html
- Source role: Primary policy and declared legal/operational position.
- Provenance note: The strategy says Japan will retain an exclusively defense-oriented policy, not become a threatening military power, and prohibit preemptive strikes. It also authorizes counterstrikes against an opponent's territory after an armed attack and calls the buildup unprecedented in scale and content. Both parts must remain visible; the declared constraint cannot erase the expanded capability, and the expanded capability alone cannot prove aggressive intent.

## Independent and plural-context sources

### NEUTRAL-1

- Publisher: Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI)
- Title: `Global military spending rise continues as European and Asian expenditures surge`
- Date: 2026-04-27
- Language: English
- URL: https://www.sipri.org/media/press-release/2026/global-military-spending-rise-continues-european-and-asian-expenditures-surge
- Source role: Independent, standardized expenditure context.
- Provenance note: SIPRI reports that Japan's military expenditure rose 9.7% in real terms to USD62.2 billion in 2025, or 1.4% of GDP, while China's rose 7.4% to USD336 billion, continuing a 31-year sequence of annual increases. This supports the reality of Japan's rapid growth and Japan's claim that the regional context includes other buildups. It does not prove either government's intent narrative. SIPRI's methodology differs from national budget accounting, so do not directly equate its USD total with Japan's JPY9 trillion inclusive budget measure.

### PLURAL-1

- Publisher: Research Committee on Foreign Affairs and National Security, House of Councillors, National Diet of Japan
- Title: `Research Report on Foreign Affairs and National Security (Interim Report) (Excerpt)`
- Date: 2023-06
- Language: English provisional translation
- URL: https://www.sangiin.go.jp/eng/report/2023gaikouanzenhosho.pdf
- Source role: Official parliamentary research record preserving competing Japanese views rather than a single executive position.
- Provenance note: The report records arguments that counterstrike capabilities may deter attack, concerns about priorities and preemption, arguments that front-line missile deployment may harm Japanese security, and calls for deeper debate about whether higher spending and missile deployment preserve peace. It supports the existence of legitimate domestic disagreement and is a strong adversarial re-check source. It does not establish how prevalent each view is because it summarizes opinions rather than a representative survey.

## Legal baselines

### LAW-1

- Publisher: House of Representatives, National Diet of Japan
- Title: `The Constitution of Japan`
- Promulgated: 1946-11-03; effective 1947-05-03
- Language: English
- URL: https://www.shugiin.go.jp/internet/itdb_english.nsf/%20html/statics/english/constitution_e.htm
- Source role: Authoritative domestic constitutional text.
- Provenance note: Article 9 renounces war and the threat or use of force as a means of settling international disputes, and says war potential will not be maintained. The text is necessary but not sufficient to resolve the modern constitutional interpretation of counterstrike capabilities.

### LAW-2

- Publisher: United Nations
- Title: `United Nations Charter (full text)`
- Signed: 1945-06-26; entered into force 1945-10-24
- Language: English
- URL: https://www.un.org/en/about-us/un-charter/full-text
- Source role: Authoritative international-law baseline.
- Provenance note: Article 51 preserves individual and collective self-defense after an armed attack. It does not establish that any specific counterstrike plan or execution would satisfy necessity, proportionality, reporting, or other applicable legal requirements.

## Recommended claim-level expected findings

These are fixture expectations, not immutable product verdicts.

| Claim layer | Suggested finding | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| FY2026 budget exceeded JPY9 trillion and was a record | `supported` | Japan's own budget material confirms it, with an accounting-scope qualification. |
| Japan is rapidly expanding defense capabilities | `supported_with_qualification` | Spending growth, long-range stand-off systems, unmanned systems, and counterstrike policy support the claim; "rapid" should be tied to a period and metric. |
| Japan now possesses or is acquiring counterstrike capability against an opponent's territory | `supported` | Japan's own strategy expressly states this, subject to declared use conditions. |
| The buildup exceeds exclusively defense-oriented policy | `contested_interpretation` | China says yes; Japan says no; capability and constitutional texts establish the dispute but do not by themselves settle it. |
| The policy breaches the Constitution or postwar international order | `insufficient_evidence` or `contested_interpretation` | A legal conclusion requires a defined legal test and authoritative legal analysis; Article 9 and Article 51 alone do not resolve it. |
| Japanese right-wing forces intend to revive militarism | `unsupported` | The evidence demonstrates capability and policy change, not a plan to restore the pre-1945 political-military system. Historical concern is legitimate context but not proof of current intent. |
| "Self-defense" terminology is intended to deceive | `unsupported` | Requires direct evidence of deceptive intent; disagreement with the characterization is not enough. |
| Critical accounts are intentional information warfare against Japan | `unsupported` | Apply the same intent standard to the Japanese minister's reciprocal allegation. Some accounts may omit regional context without thereby proving coordinated or deceptive intent. |
| Higher Japanese spending necessarily reduces regional peace | `causal_overreach` | SIPRI recognizes broader regional rearmament and insecurity, but this evidence set cannot isolate the budget's net causal effect on deterrence versus arms-race risk. |
| Japanese domestic criticism exists | `supported_with_qualification` | The Diet report records real competing views; it does not show majority opinion or the scale of protest. |

## Falsification questions for the static sample

1. What accounting definition and comparison series would make the JPY9 trillion claim misleading?
2. What observable policy, organization, doctrine, or conduct would distinguish a large defensive buildup from revival of pre-1945 militarism?
3. What direct evidence would establish the alleged intent to deceive or conduct information warfare?
4. Under which domestic and international legal tests could counterstrike capability remain defensive, and what facts would cause it to fail those tests?
5. Are Japan's stated restrictions operationally binding, independently reviewable, and reflected in procurement and command arrangements?
6. Does regional context explain Japan's buildup, or is it being used to avoid evaluating the marginal risks created by Japan's own choices?
7. What evidence supports deterrence benefits, and what evidence supports security-dilemma or arms-race harms?
8. How representative are the cited Japanese protests, scholars, and parliamentary opinions?

## Recommended `Challenge this finding` behavior

Target the initial finding on the historical analogy or exclusively-defense-oriented policy, not the uncontested budget number.

Suggested initial finding:

> Japan is undertaking a rapid and material defense buildup, including counterstrike capabilities. The cited evidence does not establish an intent to revive pre-1945 militarism or deceive the public, and the policy's compatibility with exclusively defense-oriented doctrine remains contested.

Suggested challenge prompt:

> Search for evidence that weakens Falsify's conclusion that the militarism analogy is unsupported, including Japanese domestic legal criticism, operational features that blur defense and offense, and official statements inconsistent with the declared limits.

Expected revision using the static set:

- keep the motive/historical-identity claim `unsupported` unless new direct evidence appears;
- strengthen the qualification that counterstrike capability is a major policy break and that credible Japanese voices dispute its deterrent value and fit with defensive doctrine;
- preserve the distinction between a legitimate warning about erosion of restraint and a demonstrated plan to revive militarism;
- record that the conclusion was **qualified**, not simply upheld or reversed.

The reciprocal Japanese intent allegation should be separately challengeable by searching for critical reports that explicitly include Chinese and regional spending context. If such reports exist, that evidence weakens the broad claim that criticism necessarily omits surrounding military developments.

## Demo-neutrality guardrails

- Treat both governments' official pages as primary evidence of what each government said, not as neutral validation.
- Confirm the budget increase and counterstrike acquisition even if the stronger Chinese framing is not established.
- Preserve the historical basis for regional concern without turning historical analogy into proof of present intent.
- Apply the same intent threshold to China's "deception" claim and Japan's "information warfare" claim.
- Do not use China's larger expenditure as a blanket defense of Japanese policy; do not omit it from regional context either.
- Distinguish domestic constitutional interpretation, international legality, operational capability, intent, and predicted strategic effect.
- Use labels such as `supported`, `supported_with_qualification`, `contested_interpretation`, `insufficient_evidence`, `causal_overreach`, and `historical_analogy_not_established`; never emit a country-level credibility score.
